The Efficiency Question

More hours doesn't mean more gain.

The chart shows total session hours against final SAT point gain, for every student. There is no correlation between spending more time and earning more points.

Prince gained 90 points in 9 hours. Rae gained 360 in 48. Both outcomes were correct, because both started from different positions and had different ceilings to reach.

The method doesn't sell hours. It sells a ceiling. The path to that ceiling is calibrated to each student, and it ends when there's nothing left to build.

Every student receives a projected score at intake, based on diagnostic performance and program length. That number is a commitment.

92% of students met or exceeded that number on official test day. The one student who didn't landed 20 points below projection — still a 240-point improvement from where he started.

The range of student profiles matters. Three students entered with learning differences and accommodations. Six were first-generation college-goers. Start scores ranged from 1030 to 1470. The method doesn't require a particular type of student to work.
